Problem 1.   Gaussian Integrals and moment generating functions

Motivation: Consider a harmonic oscillator with potential energy U⁢(x)=12⁢k⁢x2. If the harmonic oscillator is subjected to an additional constant force f in the x direction its potential energy is U⁢(x,f)=12⁢k⁢x2-f⁢x. As we will see shortly, the probability to find the harmonic oscillator coordinate between x and x+d⁢x is

P⁢(x)⁢d⁢x=C⁢e-U⁢(x,f)/kB⁢T⁢d⁢x. (1)

This motivated people to study integrals of the form

I⁢(f)≡C⁢∫-∞∞dx⁢e-12⁢x2+f⁢x (2)

where f is a real number and C is a normalizing constant.

Method: Consider integrals of the following form

In=⟨xn⟩=12⁢π⁢∫-∞∞dx⁢e-12⁢x2⁢xn (3)

which come up a lot in this course. There is a neat trick for evaluating the integrals In, known as the moment generating (or characteristic) function.

Instead of considering In, consider the average of ⟨ea⁢x⟩.

I⁢(a)≡⟨ea⁢x⟩=12⁢π⁢∫-∞∞e-12⁢x2⁢ea⁢x (4)

with a a fixed real number. Why would one ever want to do this? Well, if you differentiate with respect to a (under the integral sign) and then set a=0, you pull down an x:

dd⁢a⁢ea⁢x|a=0=ea⁢x⁢x|a=0=x. (5)

Thus, we may differentiate under the integral sign and find ⟨x⟩ from I⁢(a):

dd⁢a⁢I⁢(a)|a=0=12⁢π⁢∫-∞∞e-12⁢x2⁢x=⟨x⟩ (6)

The trick can be repeated any number of times. For instance since

(dd⁢a)4⁢ea⁢x|a=0=ea⁢x⁢x4|a=0=x4 (7)

We have

(dd⁢a)4⁢I⁢(a)|a=0=12⁢π⁢∫-∞∞e-12⁢x2⁢x4=⟨x4⟩ (8)

In summary, knowing I⁢(a) amounts to know all moments of the probability distribution

⟨xn⟩ (9)
