A.4 Estimates

(February 5, 2023)

We came across a number of physical constants that I expect you to know. Outside of this list, the constants will be given.

  1. 1.

    Avagadros number NA=6×1023.

  2. 2.

    The speed of light c=3×108⁢m/s.

  3. 3.

    The speed of sound in air is approximately cs≃330⁢m/s.

  4. 4.

    A useful unit of volume is liters. One liter is (10⁢cm)3=1000⁢cm3. One mole of an ideal gas at STP has a volume of 22⁢L.

  5. 5.

    A useful unit of pressure is a bar. One atmosphere is approximately 1 bar. 1 bar is 105⁢N/m2. A typical scale of energy is 1⁢bar×1⁢L=100⁢J.

  6. 6.

    The ideal gas constant is R=8.32⁢J/K∘:

  7. 7.

    The Boltzmann constant kB you can remember in two ways:

    • •

      The macroscopic way: one Avogadro’s number times kB is R:

      NA⁢kB=R (A.14)
    • •

      The microscopic way: k⁢T is one fortieth of an eV at T=300⁢K∘.

      kB=140⁢eV300⁢K∘=0.025⁢eV300⁢K∘ (A.15)
  8. 8.

    You should remember the proton (and neutron mass) in two ways:

    • •

      The microscopic way: i.e. the rest energy in mega electron volts is

      mp⁢c2≃938⁢MeV≃1000⁢MeV (A.16)
    • •

      The macroscopic way: an Avagadro’s number of protons weighs a gram. This is the molar mass of the proton:

      ℳml=mp⁢NA=1⁢g (A.17)

    Protons and neutrons and weigh nearly the same thus the mass of one Avagadro’s number of diatomic oxygen weighs 32⁢g, since there are eight protons and neutrons in one oxygen nucleus, and two such nuclei. The electrons are light (see below) for the mass budget.

    You might want to use either of these methods to evaluate vrms in atomic hydrogen gas at room temperature

    vrms=3⁢k⁢Tmp=c⁢3⁢k⁢Tmp⁢c2=(3×108⁢m/s)×(1/40)⁢eV938×106⁢eV≃2700⁢m/s (A.18)

    Or if you prefer

    vrms=3⁢kB⁢Tm=3⁢(NA⁢kB)⁢TNA⁢mp=3⁢R⁢T1⁢g=3×8.32⁢J/K∘×300⁢K∘1⁢g≃2700⁢m/s (A.19)

    This is almost a factor of 10 faster than the speed of sound cs≃330⁢m/s, because hydrogen is so light.

  9. 9.

    You should remember the electron mass in two ways:

    • •

      The microscopic way: the mass is “half an MeV”

      me≃0.5⁢MeV (A.20)
    • •

      In comparison to the proton mass:

      memp≃12000 (A.21)
  10. 10.

    Plancks constant is needed to convert wavelength to energy

    ℏ⁢c=197⁢eV⁢nm (A.22)

    or using h=2⁢π⁢ℏ

    h⁢c=1240⁢eV⁢nm (A.23)

    Thus the energy of a photon of yellow light with λ=550⁢nm (emitted by sodium) is

    E=h⁢cλ=1240⁢e⁢V⁢nm550⁢nm≃2.3⁢eV (A.24)

    Planks constant is also useful for measuring typical de Broglie wavelength at room temperature

    hmp⁢k⁢T=h⁢c(mp⁢c2)⁢(k⁢T)≃1240⁢eVnm106⁢eV⁢140⁢eV≃2.5⁢Å (A.25)
  11. 11.

    A useful unit of distance in atomic physics is angstroms, 1⁢Å=0.1⁢nm. The Bohr radius

    a0=0.53⁢Å (A.26)

    is about half an Angstrom. A typical bond length is normally between 1-5 Bohr Radii. (For N2 the distance between the two nuclei is 1.09⁢Å)

  12. 12.

    An electron volt is a good unit of microscopic energy. Avagadro’s number times 1⁢eV is a good unit of macroscopic chemical energy and is 100 kilo Joules.

    NA⁢eV≃100⁢kJ (A.27)

    This is sometimes called the Faraday constant22 2 We can quickly find the charge in Coulombs from this relation, 1⁢e⁢V≃100⁢kJ/NA≃1.6×10-19⁢J  and so  1⁢e=1.6×10-19⁢C. (A.28) . An explosion involves roughly an Avogadro’s number of atomic transitions, with each atomic transition releasing about an electron volt of energy, for approximately 100⁢kJ of energy per mole. Burning a mole of gasoline gives roughly this amount of energy.

  13. 13.

    The Bohr model provides a lot of estimates. In the Bohr model the radius of the lowest electron orbit is a0=0.53⁢Å, and the angular momentum of the lowest orbit is L=p⁢a0=ℏ. So the momentum is

    p=ℏa0 (A.29)

    In the lowest orbit the kinetic energy is half of the magnitude of the potential energy 33 3 This follows from Newton’s Law for a circular orbit for an electron attracted to the proton via the Coulomb force: m⁢v2r=e24⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢r2 . The potential energy is negative reflecting the positive charge +e of the proton and negative charge -e of the electron. The binding energy of the electron is

    BE= KE+PE (A.30)
    = p22⁢m+(-e24⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢a0) (A.31)
    = -13.6⁢eV (A.32)

    Thus we have for the Hydrogen atom where p=ℏ/a0

    K⁢E=12⁢|P⁢E|=|B⁢E| (A.33)
    ℏ22⁢me⁢a02=12⁢(e24⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢a0)≃13.6⁢eV (A.34)

    13.6⁢eV is the Rydberg constant. For the ground states of a quantum mechanical system it is often the case that

    K⁢E∼|P⁢E|∼|B⁢E| (A.35)

    These facts gives another way to estimate the thermal de Broglie wavelength of a proton, by inserting a0 and the electron mass

    λth∼ℏ2⁢mp⁢k⁢T=a0⁢ℏ22⁢me⁢a02⁢(1kB⁢T)⁢memp=0.5⁢Å⁢13.6⁢eVkB⁢T⁢12000 (A.36)
