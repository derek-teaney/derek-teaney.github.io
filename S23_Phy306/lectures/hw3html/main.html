Problem 1.   Distributions on the sphere

  1. (a)

    Consider a particle with angular coordinates (θ,ϕ) which are uniformly distributed over a sphere of radius R, i.e. the probability per area is constant. Briefly explain (using the definition of solid angle) why d⁢𝒫=C⁢d⁢Ω with C a normalizing constant. Determine C and show that the probability distribution per d⁢θ is d⁢𝒫=12⁢sin⁡θ⁢d⁢θ. Show that ⟨cos2⁡θ⟩sphere=13.

  2. (b)

    Consider a particle which is distributed (not uniformly) over the sphere

    d⁢𝒫=C⁢(1+cos2⁡θ)⁢d⁢Ω4⁢π (1)

    where C is a normalizing constant. This probability distribution is visualized in Fig. 1. Show that C=3/4. Then show that the probability distribution in Eq. (1), implies that the probability distribution for u=cos⁡(θ) is

    d⁢𝒫=38⁢(1+u2)⁢d⁢u (2)

    for u∈[-1,1].

  3. (c)

    Show for the probability distribution of (b) that ⟨cos2⁡θ⟩sphere=25 by averaging ⟨u2⟩ with the distribution in Eq. (2).

Two takeaway morals:

  • •

    Moral 1: 4⁢π=2⋅2⁢π, the spherical measure factorizes into two properly normalized probability distriubtions for θ and ϕ:

    d⁢Ω4⁢π=sin⁡θ⁢d⁢θ⁢d⁢ϕ4⁢π=P⁢(θ)⁢d⁢θ⁢P⁢(ϕ)⁢d⁢ϕ=(12⁢sin⁡θ⁢d⁢θ)⁢(d⁢ϕ2⁢π) (3)

    So if you don’t care abount the about the azimuthal direction and only want to know about the probability of θ, one has the measure d⁢𝒫=12⁢sin⁡θ⁢d⁢θ.

  • •

    Moral 2: When integrating over the sphere it is almost always better to integrate over u=cos⁡θ, so that the measure is flat d⁢Ω=d⁢u⁢d⁢ϕ.

Problem 3.   Probability distribution of a Quantum Harmonic Oscillator

Consider a quantum harmonic oscillator in one dimension interacting with a thermal environment. This could be, for example, a single atom attached via a spring-like bond to a large stationary molecule. The vibrational frequency of the oscillator is ω0=k/m.

Recall that the energy levels of a quantum harmonic oscillator are

ϵn=(n+12)⁢ℏ⁢ω0 (9)

with n=0,1,2,…. ℏ⁢ω0 is one quantized unit of vibrational energy. The integer n is known as the vibrational quantum number – the larger is n, the larger is the energy (in units of ℏ⁢ω0, and the more the atom is vibrating. For large n the energy is nearly continuous and the motion can be treated classically. A plot of the energy levels and the wave-functions are shown in Fig. 2.

Since only differences in energies are physically important, we can shift what we call “zero energy” downward by 12⁢ℏ⁢ω0 and write the energy levels as

ϵn=n⁢ℏ⁢ω0 (10)

The probability to find the oscillator in the n-th vibrational state is

Pn=1Z⁢e-ϵn/kB⁢T=1Z⁢e-n⁢ℏ⁢ω0/kB⁢T (11)

where Z is a normalizing constant. Z is known as the partition function in English, and the letter Z is short for Zustandsumme in German (meaning sum over states).

  1. (a)

    For the probabilities to be correctly normalized, show that Z=∑n=0∞e-n⁢β⁢ℏ⁢ω0. I find the German name descriptive, because, as you should see, it is a “sum over states”.

  2. (b)

    Noting that e-n⁢ℏ⁢ω0/kB⁢T=un with u=e-ℏ⁢ω0/kB⁢T, evaluate the sum, and determine the explicit form for Z and Pn. A plot of Pn for a variety of ℏ⁢ω0 is shown in Fig. 3. Describe qualitatively what this plot is telling you physically.

    You should find

    Pn=e-n⁢ℏ⁢ω0/kB⁢T⁢(1-e-ℏ⁢ω0/kB⁢T) (12)
  3. (c)

    The first vibrational excited state (n=1) of diatomic hydrogen H2 is excited from the ground state (n=0) by a photon of wavelength of λ0=2270⁢nm. This wavelength is typical of molecular vibrations – is the photon, ifrared, visible, or uv? Express the vibrational unit of energy ℏ⁢ω0 in eV. By looking at Fig. 3, give a rough estimate for the temperature when the mean number of vibrational quanta of energy in the oscillations of H2 becomes of order unity.

    Please do use constants like ℏ=1.05×10-34⁢J⁢s and kB=1.38×10-23⁢J/K-1∘, but rather try to put it in physical terms. For instance use ℏ⁢c=197⁢eV⁢nm, and kB⁢T≃1/44⁢eV at freezing 273⁢K∘. A summary of constants you will need for the course are given on the website.

    Discussion: I find T≃6000⁢Ko. Indeed this is about right. At room temperatures diatomic molecules translate and rotate so ⟨ϵ⟩=52⁢kB⁢T. At higher temperatures (at around 1000⁢Ko) they begin to vibrate, and the 52⁢kB⁢T estimate for the molecule’s energy is no longer valid. Much above this temperature the molecule begins to break apart as the number of vibrational quanta gets larger and larger. The following figure shows the specific heat of H2 gas. As we will discuss fully later, the increase increase starting at around 1000⁢Ko reflects the fact vibrational degrees of freedom becoming active, i.e. that the mean n is becoming greater than one.
