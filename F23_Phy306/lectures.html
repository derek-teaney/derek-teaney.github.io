Basic Thermodynamics

  • Estimates of ideal gasses
    • Ideal Gas 1: Ideal gas law, basic constants, and the equipartition theorem.
    • Ideal Gas 2: Estimates of interparticle spacing, typical speed, debroglie wavelength.
  • The First Law and Work
    • Work, the first law, and isothermal expansions. Also discusses the difference between an inexact differential (an small amount dW and dQ), versus an exact differential (a small change dE).
  • Specific Heats
    • Specific heats describe how energy entering the system changes the temperature. We parametrize this with specific heats Cv and Cp. I discuss these quantities and some measurements of them for gasses and solids.
  • Engines
    • The adiabatic expansion
    • Engines. Describes the Otto Cycle. The carnot enegine is also briefly described. In class we presented slides about how the car engine actually works, see slides. You should look at these slides while you read the notes on the Otto cycle, to try and make it a bit less abstract.
  • Non Ideal Gasses and general substances
    • The equation of state are the functions P(T,V) or V(T,P). The expansion coefficients and the isothermal compressibility parametrize the V(T,P) relation. At the end, I give two formulas, who's significance can be understood here, but which can be proved only later. The first relates the adiabatic compressibility to the isothermal one. The second relates Cv and Cp for a general substance.
    • The energy function Describes the energy of a system U(T,V) how it depends on temperature and volume. The specific heats tell one a lot about this function.
  • Enthalpy
    • Discusses changes in phase, interpreting the Latent Heat as the change in Enthalpy of the system. Discusses chemical reactions in atmosphere and interprets the heat released in terms of change in enthalpy Lecture

          • Entropy and the Second Law

          Entropy and the Microcanonical Ensemble
          • Entropy. Gives an introduction to entropy based on N indpendent quantum harmonic oscillators. Based on Chapter 4 as well as my own perspective.
          • The microcanonical ensemble. Shows how the relation between temperature cand energy an be found by finding the entropy in a concrete example of N independent quantum oscillators. We will find the temperature by directly counting the number of ways to share the energy.
          • The Boltmann factor. Derives the Boltzmann factor from notions of entropy. This is based on 4.3 and 4.4. This completes the connection between the microcanonical ensemble (i.e. directy counting of ways to share the energy) and the canonical ensemble (i.e. using the Boltzman factor and partition functions).

          Entropy and Heat
          • Entropy and Irreversibility. Discusses entropy and irreversibility.
          • Entropy and Heat Flow. Works through several examples of entropy associated with heat flow. Readings: Chapter 14.1, 14.2
          • Entropy, Pressure, Mechanical Equilibrium. Discusses mechanical equilibrium (equalities of pressures), in equilibrium. Readings: Chapter 14.3
          • Entropy of an Ideal Gas
            • Thermodynamic Approach. Computes the entropy of a classical mono-atomic gas from thermodynamics.
            • Statistical Approach. Computes the entropy of a classical mono-atomic gas, by directly counting microstates. Not in our book, but in most others at this level e.g. Schroeder Ch. 2
          • Entropy Change in free expansion. Works through entropy change of a free expansion. Readings: Chapter 14.4
          • Entropy of mixing. Entropy change due to mixing of two gasses. Readings: Chapter 14.6
          • Entropy and Carnot Efficiency. Discusses Carnot efficiency using entropy concepts. Shows that in a non-equilibrium process the entropy produced is greater than dQ/T.
          • (Not included) Entropy of ideal gas from a mathematical perspective
            • Exact and Inexact Differentials, Blundell, appendix C.7 and Example 11.1
            • Heat and Entropy as inexact and exact differentials, This allows us to revisit the Carnot Cycle.

            • Entropy, Probability, and the Canonical Ensemble
            • Entropy in the canonical ensemble. Determines the entropy of a subsystem using partition functions. The total entropy is a sum of the subsystems. Readings 14.8
